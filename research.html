Working Paper · Theory + Experiment

Signalling in Two-Sided Matching Markets

with Dr. Siqi Pan · 2024–Present

Centralised matching markets rely on candidate-proposing deferred acceptance to guarantee strategy-proofness and candidate-optimal outcomes. This paper studies whether pre-match messaging by firms undermines these guarantees. We build a model incorporating ego-utility, reciprocity, rejection aversion, and rank-dependent preferences, and test its predictions in a laboratory experiment with committed, uncommitted, and random-message treatments. Firms direct messages strategically, candidates respond by mis-reporting their rank-order lists in favour of the messaging firm, and market outcomes shift toward firm-optimal stable matchings. Pre-match communication is an accessible, low-risk manipulation that exploits behavioural biases strategy-proof mechanisms were not designed to withstand.

Main results

  • Candidates mis-report: Behavioural biases lead candidates to misreport preferences toward messaging firms.
  • Firms message strategically: Firms target firm-optimal partners to manipulate candidate behaviour.
  • Outcomes shift toward firms: Firms gain at candidates' expense.
  • Implication: Seemingly innocuous communication can distort designer objectives — mechanism design must account for the broader strategic environment.

Information Provision in School Choice: A Theoretical Perspective

with Dr. Georgy Artemov · 2024–Present

Students from low-socioeconomic (low-SES) backgrounds often face substantial information frictions in school choice. This paper examines the welfare implications of two types of information interventions — providing students with common information (CI) and group-specific information (GI) — in a many-to-one matching model with a continuum of students and a finite set of schools. We show that while CI and GI perform similarly when adopted on a small scale, GI strictly outperforms CI when information uptake is high. At scale, CI generates congestion externalities that reduce the benefit to informed low-SES students and hurt both high-SES and uninformed low-SES students. GI eliminates this congestion. These findings also hold when the district implements affirmative action policies.

Main results

  • Small scale: Common and group-specific information deliver similar welfare gains.
  • High uptake: Group-specific information strictly dominates — common information creates congestion that erodes its own benefits.
  • Policy: Policy-makers should promote information interventions which focus on heterogeneity in student preferences.

In Progress · Field

Closing the Gap: The Role of University Financial Aid Information on Students' Education Decisions

with Dr. Alexandra de Gendre and Dr. Siqi Pan · 2022–Present

This project studies how information about university financial aid shapes the educational decisions of high-school students in Victoria, Australia. We designed a tailored Financial Aid Calculator to deliver personalised information and used it to document — and address — the information gap facing rural Australian youth. The Honours thesis underpinning this project was awarded first place in the Deakin Essay Competition (2022) and received the highest score in the Economics Honours cohort.

Main results

  • Information gap: Rural Australian youth substantially lack college-aid information.
  • Belief updating: A tailored information treatment shifted beliefs about aid accessibility and university affordability.
  • Going forward: Information provision can be a powerful tool to address educational disparities.
